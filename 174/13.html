13.1 Introduction to probability

Speaking very loosely, an event’s probability is the “chance” it will occur. There are two kinds of probabilities: empirical probability, the proportion of times an event occurred over a number of trials; and theoretical probability, the proportion of times an event should occur over the long run. We assign theoretical probabilities according to mathematical principles so that the long-run empirical probability tends towards the theoretical probability. It will not surprise you to learn, then, that probability is a function of sets.

Definition 13.1.1 Let EEE be an event contained in the universe Ω\OmegaΩ (in probability-theoretic contexts the universe is called the sample space). If every outcome of EEE is equally likely, then the probability of EEE is P(E)=∣E∣∣Ω∣.P(E) = \frac{|E|}{|\Omega|}.P(E)=∣Ω∣∣E∣​.

To brush some interesting technical details away, we are assuming that EEE and Ω\OmegaΩ are finite sets and doing discrete probability with something called the “counting measure.” There are other ways to measure sets, and those measures have associated probabilities as well; but this is beyond our scope.

Sometimes it is desirable to know how one event affects another. For example: does the first roll being even make it more or less likely that the sum will be prime? Or is there no effect? Conditional probability answers this question for us.

Definition 13.1.2 Let EEE and FFF be events. The conditional probability of EEE given that FFF is known to occur is P(E∣F)=∣E∩F∣∣F∣. P(E|F) = \frac{|E \cap F|}{|F|}.P(E∣F)=∣F∣∣E∩F∣​.

A very good interpretation of the above definition is that the original sample space is replaced with or “localized” to be FFF.

13.2 Algebraic rules

The probability that at least one of EEE or FFF occurs is denoted P(E∪F)P(E \cup F)P(E∪F), and the probability that both events occur is denoted P(E∩F)P(E \cap F)P(E∩F).

Theorem 13.2.1 If EEE and FFF are events, then P(E∩F)=P(E)P(F∣E)=P(F)P(E∣F)P(E \cap F) = P(E)P(F|E) = P(F)P(E|F)P(E∩F)=P(E)P(F∣E)=P(F)P(E∣F) and P(E∪F)=P(E)+P(F)−P(E∩F).P(E \cup F) = P(E) + P(F) - P(E \cap F).P(E∪F)=P(E)+P(F)−P(E∩F).

Proof. Fundamental counting principles, together with the observation that one of EEE or FFF has an effect on the other. □\square□

Definition 13.2.2 If P(E∩F)=0P(E \cap F) = 0P(E∩F)=0, the events EEE and FFF are disjoint.

Example. The events “the first roll is odd” and “the first roll is even” are disjoint since they cannot both happen at the same time.

Theorem 13.2.3 The following are equivalent.

  • P(E∩F)=P(E)P(F)P(E \cap F) = P(E)P(F)P(E∩F)=P(E)P(F)
  • P(E∣F)=P(E)P(E|F) = P(E)P(E∣F)=P(E)
  • P(F∣E)=P(F)P(F|E) = P(F)P(F∣E)=P(F)
  • EEE and FFF are independent. (This is a definition of the other three bullets.)

Example. Any event involving just the first die and any event involving just the second die will be independent.

Takeaways

  • Probability is in some sense the study of relative measures of sets.
  • When the elements of the sample space can be enumerated, this is usually the correct approach.
  • If not, the addition and multiplication principles lift to rules about probability (with the added wrinkle of conditional probability).
