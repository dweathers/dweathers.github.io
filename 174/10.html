10. Ordered samples

Review.

  • Chapter 6: sequences

Many of the things we are trying to count can be conceptualized as samples being taken from a sample space (think universal set). Whether that sample is ordered or unordered, and whether the elements may be repeated or replaced, affects the way the samples should be counted.

In this chapter we will discuss samples where the order of the elements is relevant. Examples include “words” (strings of characters that may have meaning attached, whether those characters be the usual letters or something else) and groups of people where each person has a different role (so switching the people in their roles would create a different configuration of the team).

Ordered samples with replacement are finite sequences, and ordered samples without replacement are permutations.

10.1 Finite sequences

Let nnn and rrr be natural numbers and Ω\OmegaΩ be a set of nnn elements. Interchangeably we will call Ω\OmegaΩ the universe or sample space (in the context of this particular section Ω\OmegaΩ may also be called the alphabet).

A finite sequence in Ω\OmegaΩ is exactly what it sounds like: an ordered sample (ak)k=1r=(a1,a2,…,ar) (a_k)_{k=1}^r = (a_1,a_2,\ldots, a_r)(ak​)k=1r​=(a1​,a2​,…,ar​) where each aka_kak​ may be the same. We say that the elements may be “repeated” or, if they are concrete objects being drawn or selected, “replaced.” (Imagine a Scrabble tile being put back in the bag, or a card back in the deck.)

Given Ω\OmegaΩ, how many sequences of length rrr are in Ω\OmegaΩ? Let’s consider the following example.

Example. Suppose a password of length 101010 is to be made of only lowercase letters. The alphabet, or universe, is the set {a,b,c,…,z}\{a, b, c, \ldots, z\}{a,b,c,…,z}. We must choose a letter for the first character of the password: there are 262626 total choices. Then we must choose the second character. Since usually passwords allow for character repetition, there are still 262626 choices; and for the third, and fourth, so on. Hence there are 26×26×⋅×26=2610 26 \times 26 \times \cdot \times 26 = 26^{10}26×26×⋅×26=2610 total passwords. (See the problems at the end of the chapter for why you should vary the types of characters in your passwords.)

10.2 Permutations

Example. Suppose a team of three people, each with a different role, is to be made from seven volunteers. Are there 737^373 possible teams? No, because this formula assumes that one person could take all three roles. That would not be a “team of three people.” Usually when we are counting groups of people, we are not allowing for repetition.

In the last chapter, we defined a permutation of Ω\OmegaΩ to be an ordering of its elements, and discovered that there are n!n!n! such orderings if Ω\OmegaΩ has nnn elements. In the above example, there are 7!7!7! ways to arrange the entire set of volunteers. That’s closer to what we want, but we still only need three of the volunteers.

Consider that there are 777 choices for the first role. Now that person may not be chosen again, and there are only 666 choices for the second role. Accordingly, there are only 555 choices for the third role. Altogether according to the multiplication principle, we have 7×6×5=210 7 \times 6 \times 5 = 2107×6×5=210 options.

There is another way to come up with the number 7×6×57 \times 6 \times 57×6×5. Remember that there are 7!7!7! ways to arrange the whole set of people, but that we only care about the arrangement of three of them. In other words, there are four people whose arrangement we don’t care about at all. What if we divided 7!7!7! by the 4!4!4! ways to arrange those people? 7!4!=7×6×5=210 \frac{7!}{4!} = 7 \times 6 \times 5 = 210 4!7!​=7×6×5=210 That’s the same answer! How convenient…

Takeaways

  • Ordered samples with replacement are called finite sequences and there are nrn^rnr of them.
  • Ordered samples without replacement are called rrr-permutations and there are n!/(n−r)!n!/(n-r)!n!/(n−r)! of them.
