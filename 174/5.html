5. Recursion and integer representation

One of the most important ideas in discrete mathematics is that of recursion, which we will explore in one way or another for many of the remaining chapters in the course. In this chapter, we will explore it through the idea of integer representation; representing integers in bases other than the common base 10. Bases you are likely to encounter in computer science contexts are base 2 (binary), base 8 (octal), and base 16 (hexadecimal). We will learn how to represent an integer in binary using a recursive algorithm, and then we will cheat our way into octal and hexadecimal.

5.1 Recursion

Recursion is when an algorithm, object, or calculation is constructed recursively.

Just kidding. Reread this joke after the chapter and it’ll make more sense.

Recursion means that an algorithm, object, or calculation is written, defined, or performed by making reference to itself. It is something more easily understood by example, and there will be plenty as we proceed. For now, consider the following example.

Definition 5.1.1 Let nnn be a positive integer. Then n!n!n! ("nnn factorial") is the product of all positive integers at most nnn: n!=1×2×⋯×n=n!n! = 1 \times 2 \times \cdots \times n = n!n!=1×2×⋯×n=n! Furthermore, we define 0!=10!=10!=1.

Anyone questioning the usefulness of n!n!n! will have their questions answered in excruciating detail over the course of Chapters 9-13. If the reader has had calculus, then they may recognize n!n!n! as the denominator of the terms in the Taylor expansion of the function exe^xex; it is good for “absorbing” constants created by taking derivatives. However, we are not interested in any of its uses right now.

Example. Here are the values of a few factorials: 0!=10!=10!=1, 1!=11!=11!=1, 2!=2×1=22!=2\times 1 = 22!=2×1=2, 3!=3×2×1=63!=3\times 2 \times 1 = 63!=3×2×1=6, 4!=4×3×2×1=244!=4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 244!=4×3×2×1=24, and 5!=5×4×3×2×1=1205!=5\times 4\times 3 \times 2 \times 1=1205!=5×4×3×2×1=120.

You may have already noticed the recursion at work. Take for instance 5!=5×4×3×2×15!=5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 15!=5×4×3×2×1. We see the value of 4!4!4! lurking here: 5!=5×4!5!=5 \times 4!5!=5×4!.

In fact, for all positive nnn, we can make the alternative (usually more useful) definition n!=n(n−1)!n!=n(n-1)!n!=n(n−1)!. This is the recursive defintion of the factorial, because we are defining the factorial in terms of the factorial.

Why is this okay? Well, we’re really collapsing a lot of work into two lines: it may make more sense to break it down one step at a time. First, define 0!=10!=10!=1. This definition will make sense in a later chapter, but remember that mathematical definitions don’t necessarily need to “make sense.”

Next, define 1!=1×0!1!=1 \times 0!1!=1×0!. This definition is valid, because the number 111, the operation ×\times×, and the number 0!0!0! have all been defined. And when we go to define 2!=2×1!2!=2 \times 1!2!=2×1!, we have already defined all of these objects as well. And so on. Therefore, the following definition is a valid one.

Definition 5.1.2 Let nnn be a natural number. Then n!n!n! is equal to 1 when n=0n=0n=0, and n(n−1)!n(n-1)!n(n−1)! otherwise.

5.2 Binary numbers

Now that we know how recursion works (or at least have more of an inkling than we did upon starting the chapter), we will see another example of it in the calculation of the binary representation of a number.

When we speak of the number one thousand five hundred eight or 1,5081,5081,508, we are implicitly talking about the composition of the number in terms of powers of 10: 1,508=1×103+5×102+0×101+8×100,1,508 = 1\times 10^3 + 5 \times 10^2 + 0 \times 10^1 + 8 \times 10^0,1,508=1×103+5×102+0×101+8×100, remembering that b0=1b^0=1b0=1 for all nonzero bbb.

African and Asian mathematicians were writing in base-10, or decimal notation, as early as 3,000 BCE. Presumably the prevalence of decimal notation throughout human history is due to the fact that most of us have ten fingers and ten toes, making the base convenient. However, computers only have two fingers and two toes.

That last line is a joke, but here’s the idea: Computers read information by way of electrical signals, which are either on or off. So the simplest way for a computer to process information is in terms of two states. Here is a higher-level example: suppose you are writing some code. Is it easier to have a single if-then statement (two possible states), or nine (ten possible states)? This is why base-2, or binary numbers, are so prevalent in computer science.

The first step to becoming adept with binary numbers is to know many powers of 222. Fortunately, powers of 222 are easy to calculate: simply multiply the last value by 222 to get the next one. (This is a recursive definition for 2n2^n2n!)

This examples was made much more tedious than it needed to be so that the recursive steps were laid bare. In practice, you will simply continue subtracting off powers of 222 until none of the target number remains, writing a 111 if you subtracted a particular power of 222 and a 000 if you didn’t. Since we had to subtract 1,0241,0241,024, 256256256, 128128128, 646464, 323232, and 444, we write a 111 for the eleventh, ninth, eighth, seventh, sixth, and third digits, and a 000 everywhere else.

5.3 Octal and hexadecimal numbers

The word octal refers to the base-8 system and hexadecimal refers to the base-16 system. These two bases are useful abbreviations of binary numbers: octal numbers are used to modify permissions on Unix systems, and hexadecimal numbers are used to encode colors in graphic design.

We say “abbreviation” because of the following algorithm.

Algorithm 5.3.1 Suppose nnn is an integer with a base bbb representation and we desire to know its base bkb^kbk representation, for some integer k>1k > 1k>1. Then we may group the digits of nnn in base bbb into groups of kkk and rewrite each group as a digit in base bkb^kbk. The resulting string is the binary expansion of nnn in base bkb^kbk.

The idea is that it is easy to go from some base to a base that is a power of the original base. Consider the following example.

Example. Write the number 236236236 in octal and in hexadecimal.

The starting point will be to write 236236236 in binary. Since 236=128+64+32+8+4,236=128+64+32+8+4,236=128+64+32+8+4, its binary expansion is (11101100)2(11101100)_2(11101100)2​.

To write 236236236 in octal, we will regroup the binary digits of 236236236 by threes because 8=238=2^38=23. However, there are eight digits. No worries; we will simply add a meaningless 000 as the leftmost digit of the binary expansion. Those groups are: 011011011, 101101101, and 100100100 from left to right. In order, those numbers are 333, 555, and 444; so, 236236236 is (354)8(354)_8(354)8​.

Observe that in a base bbb number system, the digits are 0,1,2,…,b−10, 1, 2, \ldots, b-10,1,2,…,b−1. Therefore the highest digit we will see in octal is 777.

However – what about hexadecimal? Its set of digits is larger than our usual base-10 set. It is standard usage to use AAA through FFF to represent 101010 through 151515.

Decimal digit 101010 111111 121212 131313 141414 151515
Hexadecimal digit AAA BBB CCC DDD EEE FFF

Since 16=2416=2^416=24, to write 236236236 in hexadecimal we will group the digits in its binary expansion by fours this time. Those groups are 111011101110 and 110011001100, which represent fourteen and twelve respectively. So, 236=(EC)16236=(EC)_{16}236=(EC)16​.

Takeaways

  • Recursion is when something is defined or implemented in terms of its own definition or implementation. Recursion is incredibly useful both mathematically and programmatically.
  • Given a positive integer nnn, its factorial n!n!n! – the product of all positive integers at most nnn – may be recursively expressed as n(n−1)!n(n-1)!n(n−1)!. Meanwhile, 0!=10!=10!=1.
  • Binary numbers are convenient in programming. We may write a binary number by recursively writing a smaller number in binary first.
  • We may express a binary number in octal or hexadecimal by grouping its digits by threes or fours.
