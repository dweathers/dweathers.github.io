9. Introduction to combinatorics

The time has come to learn how to count. Counting problems that require more sophisticated methods than simple listing include knowing how a program’s computation time scales with more data or counting the number of objects or structures of a particular type. In this chapter we will familiarize ourselves with the basics of counting: enumeration, addition, and multiplication.

9.1 Enumeration

In blatant disregard for the previous paragraph, a powerful tool in combinatorics is enumeration: systematically listing the objects to be counted. If the target set is small and finite, enumeration is perfectly fine. When the target set is cumbersomely large, enumeration can still help give an idea of how the elements are generated so that formal rules can give their number without the need to fully list them.

Example. How many ways are there to shuffle a deck of 52 cards?

It will be impossible to fully enumerate the space of objects to be counted (we will discuss just how impossible once we have an answer). However, let’s pretend the deck has just four cards: AAA, BBB, CCC, and DDD.

Now it is possible to systematically list out all possible shuffles of the deck. Let’s arrange them into a table where each column corresponds to a different card on top of the deck. For example, we will list the two decks where ABABAB are on top; then, we will list the two decks where ACACAC is on top; then the two decks with ADADAD on top. Here is the full list of decks:

9.2 Addition and multiplication principles

In combinatorics we count the number of outcomes that lead to an event coming to pass. In the previous example, “a deck of four cards is shuffled” is the event and the outcome BCDABCDABCDA is an outcome. We may phrase this set-theoretically by saying an event is a set and that the outcomes are the elements of that set. So, BCDA∈A Deck Of Four Cards Is Shuffled. BCDA \in \text{A Deck Of Four Cards Is Shuffled}.BCDA∈A Deck Of Four Cards Is Shuffled.

Example. A particular restaurant sells many varieties of burritos. Of the selections, seven burritos are spicy and six are vegetarian. Two vegetarian burritos are spicy. How many burritos are spicy or vegetarian?

Letting SSS be the set of spicy burritos and VVV be the set of vegetarian burritos, we are looking for ∣S∪V∣=∣S∣+∣V∣−∣S∩V∣, |S \cup V| = |S| + |V| - |S \cap V|,∣S∪V∣=∣S∣+∣V∣−∣S∩V∣, which is 7+6−27+6-27+6−2 or 111111.

Example. How many ways are there to shuffle a deck of 52 cards?

Again we will consider the example of the four card deck AAA, BBB, CCC, and DDD. Some card must go first; there are four such choices. Then a card must go second, for which there are three choices. Then two, then one.

To give an example of how set theory is relevant, we may decompose the set Deck Is Shuffled\text{Deck Is Shuffled}Deck Is Shuffled into a product of four sets: Deck Shuffled=First Card×Second Card×Third Card×Fourth Card, \text{Deck Shuffled} = \text{First Card} \times \text{Second Card} \times \text{Third Card} \times \text{Fourth Card},Deck Shuffled=First Card×Second Card×Third Card×Fourth Card, where the content of (for instance) Third Card\text{Third Card}Third Card depends on the choices of the first and second cards.

By the multiplication rule, there are 4⋅3⋅2⋅14 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 14⋅3⋅2⋅1 ways to shuffle this deck. Fortunately for us this is 242424, which was the answer we got last time!

You may have noticed we have done this calculation before. The product 4⋅3⋅2⋅14 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 14⋅3⋅2⋅1 is exactly 4!4!4!. By similar reasoning, there are 52!52!52! ways to shuffle a deck of standard size: there are 525252 choices for the top card, then 515151 choices for the next card, then (etc.)

In fact the following theorem may be proven by induction.

Theorem 9.2.2 There are n!n!n! ways to order a set of nnn elements. Such an ordering is called a permutation of the set.

The first line of the following proof finally gives some intuition as to why 0!=10!=10!=1 is a good definition.

Proof. Observe that there is one ordering of the elements of ∅\varnothing∅: the empty ordering {}\{ \}{}. Since 0!=10!=10!=1, the statement is true in the base case.

Takeaways

  • The basic tools in the combinatorist’s kit are enumeration, addition, and multiplication. A very exciting fourth tool, called the generating function, will be introduced in Chapter 11.
  • Enumeration is not just listing the elements of a set; it is systematically listing them so that the set’s structure may be better understood.
  • Addition is used to count elements of “or” events and multiplication to counts elements of “and” events.
  • Factorials are big.
