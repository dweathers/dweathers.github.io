Many times we will have a mathematical object in hand but desire it to have a property it does not have. For example, suppose I have the natural numbers N\mathbf{N}N, but would like to be able to subtract these numbers. Sometimes I will be successful. For instance, 5−3=2∈N5-3=2 \in \mathbf{N}5−3=2∈N. But othertimes I will not; say, in the case of 3−5=−23-5=-23−5=−2, which is not a natural number.

Then we construct a new object, the closure of the original, that contains the same data and the minimum amount of extra data required to allow the desired property. In the case of closing N\mathbf{N}N under subtraction, the result is Z\mathbf{Z}Z, the smallest set containing both the natural numbers and the results of subtracting any two natural numbers.

A closure YYY of the set XXX under the property PPP must satisfy the following three conditions:

  1. YYY must have property PPP.
  2. X⊆YX \subseteq YX⊆Y. In other words, YYY must be a superset of XXX. (“Subset” and “superset” are converses.)
  3. For any ZZZ such that ZZZ has property PPP and X⊆ZX \subseteq ZX⊆Z, it must also be the case that Y⊆ZY \subseteq ZY⊆Z. In this sense YYY is minimal among the supersets of XXX that have PPP. (Chapter 18 will make more sense of this idea, but not specifically as it pertains to closure.)

In this chapter we will close a relation under reflexiveness, symmetry, and transitivity. The third of these will require a detour into the operation of composing relations, and finally provide the response to Chapter 14’s call.

16.1 Reflexive and symmetric closures

In this section, RRR is a relation on the set XXX. The set XXX need not be finite unless we want to talk about a matrix or a directed graph.

Example. Suppose we have the relation R={(a,b),(a,c),(b,b),(c,b)}R = \{(a,b), (a,c), (b,b), (c,b)\}R={(a,b),(a,c),(b,b),(c,b)} on {a,b,c}\{a,b,c\}{a,b,c}. To construct the reflexive closure of RRR, we must add the pairs (a,a)(a,a)(a,a) and (c,c)(c,c)(c,c). (Notice (b,b)∈R(b,b)\in R(b,b)∈R already.) So the reflexive closure of RRR is the new relation {(a,a),(a,b),(a,c),(b,b),(c,b)}. \{(a,a),(a,b),(a,c),(b,b),(c,b),(c,c)\}.{(a,a),(a,b),(a,c),(b,b),(c,b),(c,c)}.

Definition 16.1.1 Given a set XXX, the diagonal relation Δ\DeltaΔ on XXX is the relation Δ={(x,x)  ∣  x∈X}. \Delta = \{(x,x) \;|\; x \in X \}.Δ={(x,x)∣x∈X}.

The diagonal relation is the smallest reflexive relation on a set.

Definition 16.1.2 Given a relation RRR on a set XXX, the reflexive closure of RRR is the relation R∪ΔR \cup \DeltaR∪Δ.

In other words, to close a relation under reflexiveness it is only necessary to ensure it contains all the pairs in the diagonal relation.

Are these really definitions? Well, no. In the Form of discrete math textbook in the library of Heaven, the definition of closure is the one given in the preamble to the section – in this case, the smallest reflexive superset of RRR – and then it is a theorem that R∪ΔR \cup \DeltaR∪Δ is the reflexive closure. (It is also a theorem that a closure is necessarily unique, but this is easier to prove and only needs proving once for all closures.) Fortunately for us, this is not the Form of the discrete math textbook in the library of Heaven; so we will take these as definitions. Prove that they are truly what we say they are, as an exercise, if you like.

Next we will construct the symmetric closure of any relation.

Example. Again take R={(a,b),(a,c),(b,b),(c,b)}R = \{(a,b), (a,c), (b,b), (c,b)\}R={(a,b),(a,c),(b,b),(c,b)} on {a,b,c}\{a,b,c\}{a,b,c}. To construct the symmetric closure of RRR, we must add the pairs (b,a)(b,a)(b,a), (c,a)(c,a)(c,a), and (b,c)(b,c)(b,c). (As previously discussed, (b,b)(b,b)(b,b) is invertible automatically.) Therefore the symmetric closure of RRR is {(a,b),(a,c),(b,a),(b,b),(b,c),(c,a),(c,b)}. \{(a,b), (a,c), (b,a), (b,b), (b,c), (c,a), (c,b)\}. {(a,b),(a,c),(b,a),(b,b),(b,c),(c,a),(c,b)}.

Definition 16.1.3 Given a relation RRR on a set XXX, its inverse relation is the relation R−1={(y,x)  ∣  (x,y)∈R}. R^{-1} = \{ (y,x) \; | \; (x,y) \in R \}.R−1={(y,x)∣(x,y)∈R}.

Example. You have seen this before. If fff is a function that has an inverse and (x,y)∈f(x,y) \in f(x,y)∈f (meaning y=f(x)y=f(x)y=f(x)), then (y,x)∈f−1(y,x) \in f^{-1}(y,x)∈f−1 (meaning x=f(y)x=f(y)x=f(y)).

Definition 16.1.4 Given a relation RRR on a set XXX, the symmetric closure of RRR is the relation R∪R−1R \cup R^{-1}R∪R−1.

16.2 Towards a transitive closure

We can’t define a transitive closure yet, but we can probably build one. Let’s do an example that is particularly relevant at the time this book was written.

Example. In the past two weeks, Alicia has hung out with Bob. Bob and Carlos are roommates, and Davina was Carlos’s cashier at the grocery store the other day. Davina lives with her boyfriend, Erik. If Alicia gets sick, who is at risk?

The underlying relation is
R={(a,b),(b,c),(c,d),(d,e)} R = \{ (a,b), (b,c), (c,d), (d,e) \} R={(a,b),(b,c),(c,d),(d,e)}
on the set {a,b,c,d,e}\{a,b,c,d,e\}{a,b,c,d,e} (where aaa refers to Alicia, etc.), the set of all pairs (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) where xxx interacted with yyy over the past two weeks. However, this is not the relation that totally represents the spread of the illness, because if yyy was exposed to the illness in xxx and likewise zzz was exposed by xxx, then zzz could have caught something from yyy. So if (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) and (y,z)(y,z)(y,z) are in RRR, we would like (x,z)(x,z)(x,z) to be in our relation. Therefore we are looking for the transitive closure of RRR.

Transitivity is best understood visually. Draw a directed graph with vertices representing aaa, bbb, ccc, ddd, and eee, and draw arrows between aaa and bbb, bbb and ccc, ccc and ddd, and ddd and eee. These are the arrows in RRR. If our relation is to be transitive, these arrows will induce arrows between aaa and ccc, between bbb and ddd, and between ccc and eee, i.e. the arrows corresponding to the paths of length 2 in the original relation. Then we will get arrows between aaa and ddd and between bbb and eee (paths of length 3). Finally, we will draw one last arrow between aaa and eee (paths of length 4). You can verify for yourself that the relation R∗={(a,b),(a,c),(a,d),(a,e),(b,c),(b,d),(b,e),(c,d),(c,e),(d,e)} R^* = \{(a,b), (a,c), (a,d), (a,e), (b,c), (b,d), (b,e), (c,d), (c,e), (d,e)\}R∗={(a,b),(a,c),(a,d),(a,e),(b,c),(b,d),(b,e),(c,d),(c,e),(d,e)} is transitive.

If you understand that example, then you understand the idea of a transitive closure. It remains to put some formal machinery behind the idea.

16.3 Composition

Composition may be a familiar idea from algebra. Suppose you have functions fff and ggg such that f(x)=yf(x)=yf(x)=y and g(y)=zg(y)=zg(y)=z. Then you have a third function fgfgfg such that fg(x)=zfg(x) = zfg(x)=z, called the composition of fff and ggg.

You are extremely likely to have seen fgfgfg written as g∘fg\circ fg∘f before, and probably again outside of this book. We will have a material reason to prefer the fgfgfg notation very shortly. (And I prefer it for aesthetic reasons. - DW)

A composition of relations is the identical idea, but without the extra restrictions that make a relation into a function.

Definition 16.3.1 Let RRR be a relation from XXX to YYY, and SSS be a relation from YYY to ZZZ. Their composition is the relation RS={(x,z)  ∣  ∃y s.t. (x,y)∈R and (y,z)∈S}.RS = \{(x,z) \; | \; \exists y \text{ s.t. } (x,y) \in R \text { and } (y,z) \in S \}.RS={(x,z)∣∃y s.t. (x,y)∈R and (y,z)∈S}. The composition of RRR with itself nnn times is denoted RnR^nRn.

Again, elsewhere you may see RSRSRS written as S∘RS \circ RS∘R.

16.4 Transitive closure

Composition takes the pairs (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) and (y,z)(y,z)(y,z) and returns (x,z)(x,z)(x,z), so you can already imagine how this would relate to transitivity. A transitive relation is one that contains all of its self-compositions.

Theorem 16.4.1 A relation RRR on a set XXX is transitive if and only if Rn⊆RR^n \subseteq RRn⊆R for all positive integers nnn.

Therefore, the smallest transitive relation containing RRR is the union of all of the RnR^nRn. You will recall a very, very long time ago that we defined the infinite union ⋃n=1∞Xn=X1∪X2∪⋯ \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty X_n = X_1 \cup X_2 \cup \cdots n=1⋃∞​Xn​=X1​∪X2​∪⋯ to be the set of all elements that are in at least one of the XiX_iXi​, and said that this definition would be needed exactly once in the book. Well, here we are!

Definition 16.4.2 The transitive closure of a relation RRR on a set XXX is the relation R∗=⋃n=1∞Rn. R^* = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty R^n.R∗=n=1⋃∞​Rn.

Of course, in practice, we will not need to calculate infinitely many composites. You will be able to tell that there are no new pairs that would arise from a new composition.

The following comment makes it easy to conceptualize RnR^nRn. Recall a walk of length nnn is a sequence (x1,x2,…,xn+1)(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_{n+1})(x1​,x2​,…,xn+1​) where (xi,xi+1)∈R(x_i,x_{i+1}) \in R(xi​,xi+1​)∈R. We can call these nnn-walks for short.

Theorem 16.4.3 Given a relation RRR on a set XXX, the relation RnR^nRn is precisely the set of all pairs (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) such that there is an nnn-walk in RRR beginning at xxx and ending at yyy.

Example. Consider the set X={w,x,y,z}X=\{w,x,y,z\}X={w,x,y,z} and the relation R={(w,x),(x,y),(y,z),(z,y)}R = \{(w,x), (x,y), (y,z), (z,y) \}R={(w,x),(x,y),(y,z),(z,y)}. Construct R∗R^*R∗.

We will find R2R^2R2 and R3R^3R3, and the union of these with RRR will be the transitive closure. (We will verify that R4R^4R4 will yield nothing new.)

To find R2R^2R2, find all of the pairs that are “two steps away” in the original relation. You may immediately name (w,y)(w,y)(w,y) and (x,z)(x,z)(x,z) as two such pairs, but there are two less obvious pairs: (y,y)(y,y)(y,y) and (z,z)(z,z)(z,z). Recall from the last chapter that any time you have invertible pairs in a transitive relation, that symmetry must produce loops; because yyy is related to zzz and zzz is related to yyy, for the relation to be transitive yyy must be related to yyy. (Same for zzz.) Therefore R2={(w,y),(x,z),(y,y),(z,z)}.R^2 = \{(w,y), (x,z), (y,y), (z,z)\}.R2={(w,y),(x,z),(y,y),(z,z)}.

The triple composite R3R^3R3 consists of all the pairs that are “three steps away,” which at first glance is just (w,z)(w,z)(w,z). In fact, if we stopped here we would be correct about R∗R^*R∗, but we should be careful: because of the loops in R2R^2R2 there are more pairs in R3R^3R3. For example, one iteration of RRR may take xxx to yyy; another takes yyy to zzz; and a third takes zzz back to yyy. Therefore the pair (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) is in R∗R^*R∗, even though it is also in RRR. Check for yourself that R3={(w,z),(x,y),(y,z),(z,y)} R^3 = \{(w,z), (x,y), (y,z), (z,y) \}R3={(w,z),(x,y),(y,z),(z,y)} is the set of all pairs that start and end 3-paths in the original relation.

Finally, we take the union of these three sets to make R∗={(w,x),(w,y),(w,z),(x,y),(x,z),(y,y),(y,z),(z,y),(z,z)}.R^*= \{(w,x), (w,y), (w,z), (x,y), (x,z), (y,y), (y,z), (z,y), (z,z)\}.R∗={(w,x),(w,y),(w,z),(x,y),(x,z),(y,y),(y,z),(z,y),(z,z)}.

Takeaways

  • In many fields of mathematics we would like to take one object and endow it with structure it does not necessarily already have, its closure with respect to that structure. We are not violating the golden rule, “Thou shalt not change anything in math”; rather we are creating an entirely new object that contains the data of the old object.
  • The reflexive closure of a relation is constructed by taking the union with the diagonal relation.
  • The symmetric closure of a relation is constructed by taking the union with its inverse.
  • Composition is a way of combining two relations, or a relation with itself.
  • The transitive closure of a relation is constructed by taking the infinite union of a relation and all compositions with itself.
