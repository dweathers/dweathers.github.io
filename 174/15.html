15. Relations

Review.

  • Chapter 1: Sets, especially subsets and cartesian products
  • Chapter 2 and 3: Logic, particularly proofs relying on the emptiness of the empty set (e.g., “The empty set is a subset of any set.”)

As previously stated, a relation is a way to mathematically encode the idea of a relationship between objects. What does that mean? When we “encode” an idea mathematically, we are defining a mathematical object that captures the important data of that idea. Because the thing is now a mathematical object, we can do mathematics to it. If the thing has become an expression involving real numbers, it is now subject to algebra. If the thing has become a set, it is now subject to set operations, logic, proof, combinatorics, so on. One may imagine importing a package into a programming language so they have new objects to “play with” in that language.

In this chapter we will define relations, discuss various ways to represent them, and discuss some properties of interesting relations.

15.1 Basic notions and representation

Remember that the product X×YX \times YX×Y of two sets is the collection of all pairs (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) whose first component is from XXX and whose second component is from YYY. Therefore, this is a good place to start thinking about connecting two sets.

Definition 15.1.1 Let XXX and YYY be sets. A relation RRR from XXX to YYY is any subset R⊆X×YR \subseteq X \times YR⊆X×Y. We say that XXX is the domain and YYY is the codomain of YYY. When (x,y)∈R(x,y) \in R(x,y)∈R, we say "xxx is related to yyy" and may write xRyxRyxRy.

Example. Let X={x,y,z}X = \{x,y,z\}X={x,y,z} and Y={1,2}Y=\{1,2\}Y={1,2}. One of the many relations from XXX to YYY is the set {(x,1),(x,2),(y,2)}. \{ (x,1), (x,2), (y,2) \}. {(x,1),(x,2),(y,2)}. We would say (for instance) that xxx is related to 111, or that zzz is related to nothing.

Some relations have their own symbols. We will be a little sloppy and sometimes identify the statement xRyxRyxRy with the relation itself.

Example. The relation Δ={(x,x)  ∣  x∈X}\Delta=\{(x,x) \;|\; x \in X\}Δ={(x,x)∣x∈X} is the relation of all pairs (x,y)∈X×X(x,y) \in X \times X(x,y)∈X×X where x=yx=yx=y. (When the domain and codomain of a relation are the same we say it is a relation on XXX.) This relation is called the diagonal relation on the set XXX. (Exercise: Figure out why.) It may be denoted with the symbol Δ\DeltaΔ (capital delta), or it may be denoted with the statement characterizing its elements. In other words, we might say “Consider the relation x=yx=yx=y on a set XXX.”

You have seen functions in algebra, or perhaps calculus, and we have used them in various definitions. In other words, a function f:X→Yf:X \to Yf:X→Y is an assignment that pairs every element x∈Xx \in Xx∈X with a unique element f(x)∈Yf(x) \in Yf(x)∈Y. A function is a relation, subject to the extra criteria that every element of XXX is paired to exactly one element of YYY, whereas a relation can have elements of XXX related to many elements of YYY, or no elements of YYY.

If XXX and YYY are finite sets there are two convenient ways to represent a relations between XXX and YYY. Consider a relation RRR on a set XXX (meaning domain and codomain are the same set). We can also represent RRR visually with something called a directed graph or digraph for short. From this perspective, the elements of XXX are called the vertices of the graph and the pairs (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) in the relation are called edges or arrows. An arrow is drawn from one vertex to another if those vertices are related.

It is possible to draw many graphs for one relation, so why can we say “the directed graph”? Observe that any graphs will have the same arrows between the same vertices. Two graphs for which there is a 1-1 correspondence that preserves the edges are isomorphic and are functionally the same. Therefore, we say “the” directed graph for a relation.

15.2 Properties of relations

As we study relations in the remaining chapters of this book there are a few properties that will make for valuable terminology. For the remainder of the chapter, every relation has the same domain and codomain.

Definition 15.2.1 A relation RRR on a set XXX is reflexive if every element is related to itself, in other words (x,x)∈R(x,x) \in R(x,x)∈R for all x∈Xx \in Xx∈X. The pair (x,x)(x,x)(x,x) is a diagonal pair.

Definition 15.2.2 A relation RRR on a set XXX is antireflexive if no element is related to itself, in other words (x,x)∉R(x,x) \not\in R(x,x)​∈R for all x∈Xx \in Xx∈X.

So a relation is reflexive if every element is related to itself, and antireflexive if none are. A relation can be neither, if some elements are related to themselves and some aren’t! (One relation is both, but that’s an exercise.)

In all of this section’s examples you should draw a digraph for each relation yourself and make sure you agree with which properties each relation has.

Examples. Let X={a,b,c,d}X = \{a,b,c,d\}X={a,b,c,d}. Consider the relations R1={(a,b),(a,d),(b,c),(b,d),(d,b),(d,c)}, R_1 = \{ (a,b), (a,d), (b,c), (b,d), (d,b), (d,c) \},R1​={(a,b),(a,d),(b,c),(b,d),(d,b),(d,c)}, R2={(a,a),(a,d),(b,b),(b,d),(c,c),(c,d),(d,d)}, R_2 = \{ (a,a), (a,d), (b,b), (b,d), (c,c), (c,d), (d,d) \},R2​={(a,a),(a,d),(b,b),(b,d),(c,c),(c,d),(d,d)}, R3={(a,b),(b,b),(b,c),(c,d),(d,a),(d,d)},R_3 = \{(a,b), (b,b), (b,c),(c,d),(d,a), (d,d)\},R3​={(a,b),(b,b),(b,c),(c,d),(d,a),(d,d)}, as well as the full relation X×XX \times XX×X and the empty relation ∅\varnothing∅.

The relations R2R_2R2​ and X×XX\times XX×X are reflexive as they contain all pairs of the form (x,x)(x,x)(x,x). The relations R1R_1R1​ and ∅\varnothing∅ are likewise antireflexive. The relation R3R_3R3​ is neither reflexive nor antireflexive, as it contains some of these “diagonal pairs” but not all.

Definition 15.2.3 A relation RRR on a set XXX is symmetric if for all x,y∈Xx, y \in Xx,y∈X where (x,y)∈R(x,y) \in R(x,y)∈R, then (y,x)∈R(y,x) \in R(y,x)∈R. Such a pair (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) is said to be invertible.

Definition 15.2.4 A relation RRR on a set XXX is antisymmetric if for all x,y∈Xx, y \in Xx,y∈X where (x,y)∈R(x,y) \in R(x,y)∈R, then (y,x)∉R(y,x) \not\in R(y,x)​∈R unless x=yx=yx=y. Equivalently the only invertible pairs in an antisymmetric relation are the diagonal pairs.

It is crucial to understand that symmetry and antisymmetry are two ends of a spectrum, measuring how many of the pairs in the relation are invertible. If they all are, the relation is symmetric. We would like to begin the next sentence with “if none are” but this is actually impossible, because if (x,x)∈R(x,x) \in R(x,x)∈R then it is automatically invertible. In other words the fewest amount of pairs that can be inverted are the diagonal pairs. So, an antisymmetric relation is one where the only invertible pairs are on the diagonal. Finally, a relation may be neither or both.

Examples. As before let X={a,b,c,d}X = \{a,b,c,d\}X={a,b,c,d} and consider the relations R1={(a,b),(a,d),(b,c),(b,d),(d,b),(d,c)}, R_1 = \{ (a,b), (a,d), (b,c), (b,d), (d,b), (d,c) \},R1​={(a,b),(a,d),(b,c),(b,d),(d,b),(d,c)}, R2={(a,a),(a,d),(b,b),(b,d),(c,c),(c,d),(d,d)}, R_2 = \{ (a,a), (a,d), (b,b), (b,d), (c,c), (c,d), (d,d) \},R2​={(a,a),(a,d),(b,b),(b,d),(c,c),(c,d),(d,d)}, R3={(a,b),(b,b),(b,c),(c,d),(d,a),(d,d)},R_3 = \{(a,b), (b,b), (b,c),(c,d),(d,a), (d,d)\},R3​={(a,b),(b,b),(b,c),(c,d),(d,a),(d,d)}, as well as the full relation X×XX \times XX×X and the empty relation ∅\varnothing∅.

The relation X×XX \times XX×X is symmetric, because it contains all the possible pairs; so every (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) will find its (y,x)(y,x)(y,x). It is less obvious but equally true that ∅\varnothing∅ is symmetric. The definition is that every pair must be invertible; if there are no pairs then the definition is satisfied automatically.

The relations R1R_1R1​, R2R_2R2​, and R3R_3R3​ are each not symmetric because they contain a pair that is not invertible. The relation R1R_1R1​ has (a,d)(a,d)(a,d) but no (d,a)(d,a)(d,a); R2R_2R2​ has (b,d)(b,d)(b,d) but no (d,b)(d,b)(d,b); and R3R_3R3​ has (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) but no (b,a)(b,a)(b,a), for example.

Of these, R1R_1R1​ is not antisymmetric, because it has “some” symmetry (the pairs (b,d)(b,d)(b,d) and (d,b)(d,b)(d,b)). The relations R2R_2R2​ and R3R_3R3​ are antisymmetric. The empty set ∅\varnothing∅ manages to be both symmetric and antisymmetric because just as there are no pairs to invert, there are also no pairs to not invert. Vacuousness!

The final property to discuss is transitivity. You have heard this word before, both without this book (transitivity of equality is used to solve equations in algebra) and within (inclusion, implication, and divisibility are all transitive).

Definition 15.2.5 A relation is transitive if for all x,y,z∈Xx, y, z \in Xx,y,z∈X, whenever (x,y)∈R(x,y) \in R(x,y)∈R and (y,z)∈R(y,z) \in R(y,z)∈R we also have (x,z)∈R(x,z)\in R(x,z)∈R.

There are two implications of this definition. The first is that any local symmetry within a transitive relation – that is, the presence of an invertible pair – must involve a loop. That is, suppose we have (x,y)∈R(x,y) \in R(x,y)∈R and (y,x)∈R(y,x) \in R(y,x)∈R in our relation. If we replace zzz with xxx in the above definition it forces us to include the loop (x,x)(x,x)(x,x) in our relation.

The next implication involves a new definition.

Definition 15.2.6 Consider a relation RRR on the set X={x1,x2,…,xn}X = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\}X={x1​,x2​,…,xn​}. A walk of length nnn is a sequence (x1,x2,…,xn,xn+1)(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n, x_{n+1})(x1​,x2​,…,xn​,xn+1​) where (xi,xi+1)∈R(x_i, x_{i+1}) \in R(xi​,xi+1​)∈R for all 1≤i≤n1 \le i \le n1≤i≤n.

(The length of the walk is a measure of the number of arrows involved, not the number of elements in the sequence.)

The second implication is that in a transitive relation, if (xi)(x_i)(xi​) is a path beginning at x1x_1x1​ and ending at xnx_nxn​, then x1x_1x1​ must be related to xnx_nxn​.

Examples. One more time: Let X={a,b,c,d}X = \{a,b,c,d\}X={a,b,c,d} and consider the relations R1={(a,b),(a,d),(b,c),(b,d),(d,b),(d,c)}, R_1 = \{ (a,b), (a,d), (b,c), (b,d), (d,b), (d,c) \},R1​={(a,b),(a,d),(b,c),(b,d),(d,b),(d,c)}, R2={(a,a),(a,d),(b,b),(b,d),(c,c),(c,d),(d,d)}, R_2 = \{ (a,a), (a,d), (b,b), (b,d), (c,c), (c,d), (d,d) \},R2​={(a,a),(a,d),(b,b),(b,d),(c,c),(c,d),(d,d)}, R3={(a,b),(b,b),(b,c),(c,d),(d,a),(d,d)},R_3 = \{(a,b), (b,b), (b,c),(c,d),(d,a), (d,d)\},R3​={(a,b),(b,b),(b,c),(c,d),(d,a),(d,d)}, as well as the full relation X×XX \times XX×X and the empty relation ∅\varnothing∅.

The relation R1R_1R1​ is missing the edge (a,c)(a,c)(a,c), for example, the loop (b,b)(b,b)(b,b), so it is not transitive.

However, R2R_2R2​ is transitive. There are no pairs (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) and (y,z)(y,z)(y,z) missing their (x,z)(x,z)(x,z). A common mistake is to ask, “Shouldn’t we require (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) for transitivity?” but neither aaa nor bbb are actually related to each other. Both are related to ddd, but the direction of the arrow matters. (If the (b,d)(b,d)(b,d) arrow were reversed, then a (a,b)(a,b)(a,b) arrow would be necessary.)

The relation R3R_3R3​ is not transitive. It is missing many edges required for transitivity; (b,d)(b,d)(b,d) is one example. As an exercise, try to find all of the missing edges that keep R1R_1R1​ and R3R_3R3​ from being transitive.

The relation X×XX\times XX×X is transitive because it contains all possible pairs. No pairs can possibly be missing, so it is transitive “by default.” Likewise ∅\varnothing∅ is transitive because there are no pairs (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) and (y,z)(y,z)(y,z) that can fail to have their (x,z)(x,z)(x,z).

Takeaways

  • A relation takes the idea of a relationship and “makes it mathematical” so that it may be studied with the tools of mathematics.
  • A relation is a subset of the product of two sets. Relations may be represented as sets of pairs, Boolean matrices, or directed graphs.
  • A relation on a set XXX may be reflexive or antireflexive depending on how many of the elements of XXX are related to themselves.
  • A relation on XXX may be symmetric or antisymmetric depending on how many of its relationships “go the other way.”
  • A relation on XXX may be transitive depending on how information “flows” through the relation.
