7.1 Growth bounds on sequences

Definition 7.1.1 Let ana_nan​ and bnb_nbn​ be terms of sequences. We say ana_nan​ is big-O of bnb_nbn​ if there exist a real number CCC and an integer kkk such that for all n>kn > kn>k, ∣an∣≤Cbn. |a_n| \le Cb_n.∣an​∣≤Cbn​. This is another technical definition that can be hard to parse, but the idea is that eventually (after the kkk-th term of the sequence) ana_nan​ does not grow faster than bnb_nbn​. In this way, bnb_nbn​ serves as an upper bound on the growth of ana_nan​.

As we will see again in Chapter 18, there may be many upper bounds. For example in a classroom of twenty-nine students I may say “Here are fewer than thirty students” or “Here are fewer than a million students.” The first statement is obviously more informative. So often you will be asked to find the least upper bound on the growth of ana_nan​; this is usually a bnb_nbn​ that is the simplest and is itself big-O of all the other options.

Theorem 7.1.2 Any non-negative sequence is big-O of itself.

Next is the sequence (log⁡n)(\log n)(logn). In this book, log⁡n\log nlogn refers to the base-2 logarithm that you might have elsewhere seen written as log⁡2n\log_2 nlog2​n. Here are a couple of ways to conceptualize this sequence and its relevance to computer science.

  • log⁡n\log nlogn is roughly the number of times a finite sequence of length nnn can be cut in half.

PICTURE OF GRAPHS

Now we know where basic functions fit into the big-O hierarchy, but what about more complicated expressions like 50n+850n+850n+8? The following theorem will help.

Theorem 7.1.3 Let (an)(a_n)(an​) and (bn)(b_n)(bn​) be sequences.

  • If ccc is a nonzero constant, then can=O(an)ca_n = O(a_n)can​=O(an​).
  • Suppose there exists a sequence cnc_ncn​ such that an=O(cn)a_n = O(c_n)an​=O(cn​) and bn=O(cn)b_n=O(c_n)bn​=O(cn​). Then, an+bna_n+b_nan​+bn​ is big-O of cnc_ncn​ as well.
  • Suppose there exist sequences AnA_nAn​ and BnB_nBn​ such that an=O(An)a_n=O(A_n)an​=O(An​) and bn=O(Bn)b_n=O(B_n)bn​=O(Bn​). Then, anbn=O(AnBn)a_nb_n = O(A_nB_n)an​bn​=O(An​Bn​).

In plain English, this theorem says:

  • Constant multiples do not affect growth rate.
  • When adding two sequences, the larger absorbs the smaller. In other words, one must only look at the highest-order term of a sum.
  • Growth rates are multiplicative; “big-O of the product is the product of the big-O’s,” though that language is very informal.

Examples. Consider the sequence whose terms are given by the formula an=n2+5n+7a_n = n^2 + 5n + 7an​=n2+5n+7. We would like to find the simplest function bnb_nbn​ of lowest order such that an=O(bn)a_n=O(b_n)an​=O(bn​).

7.2 Other growth relationships

We will briefly mention two other growth relationships here. Recall that saying an=O(bn)a_n = O(b_n)an​=O(bn​) is to say that bnb_nbn​ is an upper bound on the growth rate of ana_nan​. Then saying an=Ω(bn)a_n = \Omega(b_n)an​=Ω(bn​) (big-Omega) is to say that bnb_nbn​ is a lower bound on the growth rate of ana_nan​, and $a_n = Θ(bn)\Theta(b_n)Θ(bn​) (big-Theta) is to say that ana_nan​ and bnb_nbn​ grow in the same way.

Definition 7.2.1 Let (an)(a_n)(an​) and (bn)(b_n)(bn​) be sequences. Then

  • an=Ω(bn)a_n = \Omega(b_n)an​=Ω(bn​) if bn=O(an)b_n = O(a_n)bn​=O(an​), and
  • an=Θ(bn)a_n = \Theta(b_n)an​=Θ(bn​) if an=O(bn)a_n = O(b_n)an​=O(bn​) and bn=O(an)b_n = O(a_n)bn​=O(an​).

Takeaways

  • “Big-O” relationships are a way to quantify when one sequence is eventually bounded in its growth by another.
  • There is a hierarchy of basic sequences that may be committed to memory.
  • Beyond the basic sequences, three rules govern the relationships between more complicated functions: constant multiples do not matter; addition is absorptive; and multiplication is, well, multiplicative.
