2. Propositional logic

In mathematics, we spend a lot of time worried about whether a sentence is true or false. This is fine in the case of a simple sentence such as “The number 222 is prime.” (That sentence is true.) But there are much more complicated sentences lurking around, and whole constellations of sentences whose truth depends on one another. (An unsolved problem in mathematics is deciding whether the Riemann hypothesis, a statement about the prime numbers, is true. Most people believe it is, and many results in the literature are true given the hypothesis. The Riemann hypothesis is going to make or break a lot of research.)

So, we are interested in tools that will allow us to judge whether complicated statements are true. Such models of truth are called logic.

2.1 Statements and connectives

Definition 2.1.1. A statement is a sentence that is either true or false.

Examples. “The number 222 is prime” and “It is perfectly legal to jaywalk” are statements. Non-statements include questions (“Is it raining?”), commands (“Do your homework”), and opinions (“Mint chocolate chip ice cream is the superior flavor”). It is important to note, not necessarily for mathematical purposes, that opinions are statements of pure taste. Claims whose truth values exist but are unknown are beliefs.

Atomic propositional statements of the type discussed in this chapter are flatly always true or false. You may think of them in the same way you think of constant functions in algebra. They are denoted with lowercase letters ppp, qqq, rrr, etc. If the only statements were atomic, logic would be boring. Fortunately, we can combine statements with connectives. In the next chapter, we will look at predicate statements whose truth values depend on an input. Likewise, these may be thought of as the non-constant functions.

Compound statements arise from combining atomic statements with connectives. They are denoted using Greek letters like φ\varphiφ (“phi”), ψ\psiψ (“psi”), and γ\gammaγ (“gamma”). The truth of a compound statement depends on the connectives involved and the truth values of the constituent statements. A tool for determining the truth of a compound statement is called a truth table.

In a truth table for the compound statement φ\varphiφ, the left side of the table is the atomic statements involved in φ\varphiφ and the right side of the table is φ\varphiφ itself. Each row gives a possible combination of truth values for the atomic statements, and the corresponding truth value for φ\varphiφ. At this point, it is easier to start introducing connectives and seeing some truth tables.

Definition 2.1.2. Let ppp be a statement. Its negation is the statement ¬p\neg p¬p, the statement that is true exactly when ppp is false and vice-versa.

The statement ¬p\neg p¬p is typically read "not ppp" or “it is not the case that ppp is true.” If we have an English rendering of ppp, we try to write ¬p\neg p¬p in a natural-sounding way that also conveys its logical structure.

Example. Suppose ppp represents the statement “It is raining.” Then, ¬p\neg p¬p stands for “It is not raining.”

Below is the truth table for ¬p\neg p¬p.

ppp ¬p\neg p¬p
TTT FFF
FFF TTT

As you can see, on the left are columns corresponding to the atomic statements involved in ¬p\neg p¬p (just ppp), and each row is a possible combination of truth values for these statements. Here is a quick, useful theorem without proof:

Theorem 2.1.3. If the compound statement φ\varphiφ involves nnn different atomic statements, then its truth table has 2n2^n2n rows.

Look familiar? We will prove this theorem when we prove the related theorem about power sets later on.

Negation is called a unary connective because it only involves one statement. Our remaining connectives are all binary connectives, as they combine two statements.

Definition 2.1.4. Let ppp and qqq be statements. Their conjunction is the statement p∧qp \wedge qp∧q, which is true only when ppp and qqq are both true.

The symbol in the conjunction is called a “wedge,” and p∧qp \wedge qp∧q is read "ppp and qqq." This definition is meant to be analogous to our English understanding of the word “and.” The statements ppp and qqq are called conjuncts.

Example. Letting ppp be “It is raining” and qqq be “I brought an umbrella,” the statement p∧qp \wedge qp∧q is “It is raining and I brought an umbrella.” The statement is true only if it is currently raining and the speaker brought an umbrella. If it is not raining, or if the speaker did not bring their umbrella, the conjunction is false.

Here is the truth table for p∧qp \wedge qp∧q:

ppp qqq p∧qp \wedge qp∧q
TTT TTT TTT
TTT FFF FFF
FFF TTT FFF
FFF FFF FFF

Notice that this table is organized intentionally, not haphazardly. The rows are divided into halves, where ppp is true in one half and then false in the other half. The rows where ppp is true are further subdivided into half where qqq is true and half where qqq is false. If there were a third letter, we would subdivide the rows again, and so on. This method of organizing the truth table allows us to ensure we did not forget a row. Our leftmost atomic letter column will always be half true followed by half false, and the rightmost atomic letter column will always alternate between true and false.

Definition 2.1.5. Let ppp and qqq be statements. Their disjunction is the statement p∨qp \vee qp∨q, which is true if at least one of ppp or qqq is true.

The symbol in the conjunction is called a “vee” and p∨qp \vee qp∨q is read "ppp or qqq." This definition is meant to be analogous to our English understanding of the word “or.” The statements ppp and qqq are called disjuncts. (Did you notice that I was able to copy and paste from an above paragraph? Conjunction and disjunction share a very similar structure.)

Example. Letting ppp be “It is raining” and qqq be “I brought an umbrella,” the statement p∨qp \vee qp∨q is “It is raining or I brought an umbrella.” This time, the speaker only needs to meet one of their conditions. They are telling the truth as long as it is raining or they brought an umbrella, or even if both statements are true.

The truth table for p∨qp \vee qp∨q follows.

ppp qqq p∨qp \vee qp∨q
TTT TTT TTT
TTT FFF TTT
FFF TTT TTT
FFF FFF FFF

Of great importance to computer science and electrical engineering, but not so much in mathematics (so it will not appear again in this book), is the exclusive disjunction (or “exclusive-or”). It is a modified disjunction where exactly one of the disjuncts must be true.

Definition 2.1.6. If ppp and qqq are statements, their exclusive disjunction is the statement p⊕qp \oplus qp⊕q, which is true if and only if exactly one of ppp and qqq is true.

The statement p⊕qp \oplus qp⊕q is read "ppp exclusive-or qqq."

Example. The statement “You can have the soup or the salad” is likely meant as an exclusive disjunction.

In mathematics, disjunctions are generally considered inclusive unless stated otherwise. (An example for us all to follow!)

Our next connective is probably the most important, as it conveys the idea of one statement implying another. As we discussed earlier in the chapter, mathematics is nothing but such statements. It is also the only connective that is not relatively easy to understand given its English interpretation. So we will begin with the truth table, and think carefully about each row.

Definition 2.1.7. If ppp and qqq are statements, the conditional statement p→qp \to qp→q is true if ppp can never be true while qqq is false.

There are many ways to read p→qp \to qp→q, including “if ppp, then qqq,” "ppp implies qqq“, and "ppp is sufficient for qqq.” The statement before the arrow is the antecedent and the following statement is the consequent. Here is the conditional’s truth table.

ppp qqq p→qp \to qp→q
TTT TTT TTT
TTT FFF FFF
FFF TTT TTT
FFF FFF TTT

That p→qp \to qp→q is true in the bottom two rows may surprise you. So, let’s consider each row separately.

Example. Let ppp be “You clean your room” and qqq be “I will pay you $10,” so that p→qp\to qp→q is “If you clean your room, then I will pay you $10.”

In the case that both ppp and qqq are true, the speaker has told the truth. You cleaned, and you were paid. All is well.

In the case that ppp is true but qqq is false, the speaker has lied. You cleaned the room, but you did not earn your money.

What if ppp is false? Well, then the speaker did not lie. The speaker said that if you clean your room you will be paid. If the room isn’t cleaned, then p→qp \to qp→q cannot be falsified, no matter where qqq is true or false.

If that explanation is not sufficient for you, reread the definition of the conditional: p→qp \to qp→q being true means that ppp cannot be true without qqq. If you are unsatisfied by the English connection, then consider p→qp \to qp→q to be a purely formal object whose definition is the above truth table.

Definition 2.1.8. If ppp and qqq are statements, the biconditional statement p↔qp \leftrightarrow qp↔q is true if ppp and qqq are both true, or both false.

The statement p↔qp \leftrightarrow qp↔q may be read as "ppp if and only if qqq, "ppp is necessary and sufficient for qqq," or "ppp is equivalent to qqq." Here is the truth table.

ppp qqq p→qp \to qp→q
TTT TTT TTT
TTT FFF FFF
FFF TTT FFF
FFF FFF TTT

Example. Let ppp be “You clean your room” and qqq be “I will pay you $10,” so that p↔qp\leftrightarrow qp↔q is “I will give you $10 if and only if you clean your room.” (The statements were reordered for readability. We will see later in the chapter that this is the same statement, but this wouldn’t work for p→qp \to qp→q.) This time, the clean room and the $10 paycheck are in total correspondence; one doesn’t happen without the other.

2.2 Connecting connectives

We are not required to use one connective at a time, of course. Most statements involve two or more connectives being combined at once. If φ\varphiφ and ψ\psiψ are statements, then so are: ¬φ\neg \varphi¬φ, φ∧ψ\varphi \wedge \psiφ∧ψ, φ∨ψ\varphi \vee \psiφ∨ψ, φ→ψ\varphi \to \psiφ→ψ, and φ↔ψ\varphi \leftrightarrow \psiφ↔ψ. This fact allows us to combine as many connectives and statements as we like.

How should we read the statement p∧q→rp \wedge q \to rp∧q→r? Should it be read "ppp; also, if qqq then rrr"? Or, “If ppp and qqq, then rrr?” Just like with arithmetic, there are conventions defining which connective to apply first: negation, then conjunction/disjunction, then implication, then bi-implication. So, "If ppp and qqq, then rrr" is correct. However, to eliminate confusion in this book we will always use parentheses to make our statements clear. Therefore, we would write this statement as (p∧q)→r(p \wedge q) \to r(p∧q)→r.

Remember that a statement involving nnn letters will have 2n2^n2n rows in its truth table. The column for the left-most statement letter should be true for half the rows then false for half the rows, and you should continue dividing the rows in half so that the right-most statement letter’s column is alternating between true and false.

Finally, it is a good idea to break a statement into small “chunks” and make a column for each “chunk” separately. Observe the following example.

Example. Write down the truth table for the statement (p∧q)→(r→¬s)(p \wedge q) \to (r \to \neg s)(p∧q)→(r→¬s). When is this statement false?

2.3 Inference and equivalence

Mathematics is all about relationships. If the statement φ→ψ\varphi \to \psiφ→ψ is always true, then it tells us that whenever φ\varphiφ is true then ψ\psiψ must be as well. If the statement φ↔ψ\varphi \leftrightarrow \psiφ↔ψ is always true, then it tells us that from the point of view of logic φ\varphiφ and ψ\psiψ are actually the same statement. That is, it is never possible for them to have different truth values.

Definition 2.3.1. A tautology is a statement that is always true, denoted TTT.

Definition 2.3.2. A contradiction is a statement that is always false, denoted FFF.

Definition 2.3.3. A rule of inference is a tautological conditional statement. If φ→ψ\varphi \to \psiφ→ψ is a rule of inference, then we say φ\varphiφ implies ψ\psiψ and write φ⇒ψ\varphi \Rightarrow \psiφ⇒ψ.

Remember that a conditional statement does not say that its antecedent and consequent are both true. It says that if its antecedent is true, its consequent must also be true.

There are many rules of inference, but we will explore two.

Definition 2.3.4. Modus ponens is the statement that says we may infer qqq from p→qp \to qp→q and ppp. Symbolically: [(p→q)∧p]→q.[(p \to q) \wedge p] \to q.[(p→q)∧p]→q.
Example. Consider the conditional statement “If you clean your room, then I will give you $10.” If the speaker is trustworthy, and we clean our room, then we may conclude we will be receiving $10.

Another example: “If a function is differentiable, then it is continuous.” (You may know the meanings of these words, but it is not important.) If this statement is true, and the function fff is differentiable, then without any further work we know that fff is continuous.

Theorem 2.3.5. Modus ponens is a rule of inference.

Notice that in the definition no claim is made that modus ponens is always true. That is up to us.

Since we know how to use truth tables, we may construct one to show that [(p→q)∧p]→q[(p \to q) \wedge p] \to q[(p→q)∧p]→q is always true. Before reading the table below, try to make your own!

TTT TTT TTT TTT TTT
TTT FFF FFF FFF TTT
FFF TTT TTT FFF TTT
FFF FFF TTT FFF TTT

Proof. The above table shows that [(p→q)∧p]→q[(p \to q) \wedge p] \to q[(p→q)∧p]→q is true no matter the truth of ppp and qqq. □\square□

Definition 2.3.6. In the context of conditional statements, transitivity is the rule that if ppp implies qqq and qqq implies rrr, then ppp implies rrr. Or, [(p→q)∧(q→r)]→(p→r).[(p \to q) \wedge (q \to r)] \to (p \to r).[(p→q)∧(q→r)]→(p→r).

The wording of the definition above should you make you suspicious that this is not the only time we will see transitivity. In fact, you may have seen it before…

Example. If you give a mouse a cookie, then he will ask for a glass of milk. If he asks for a glass of milk, then he will also ask for a straw. Therefore, if you give a mouse of cookie then he will ask for a straw.

Theorem 2.3.7. Transitivity is a rule of inference.

We could simply write down another truth table and call it a day. In fact, go ahead and make sure you can prove transitivity with a truth table. Once you’re done, we’ll try something slicker to prove transitivity.

Proof. Suppose that p→qp \to qp→q and q→rq \to rq→r are true statements, and that moreover ppp is true. By modus ponens, qqq must also be true. Then, rrr is true, by a second application of modus ponens. Therefore, if ppp is true, rrr must be also; this is the definition of p→rp \to rp→r. □\square□

Pretty cool, right? We can cite proven theorems to prove a new one.

Does it work the other way? In other words: if I know that [(p→q)∧(q→r)]⇒(p→r)[(p \to q) \wedge (q \to r)] \Rightarrow (p \to r)[(p→q)∧(q→r)]⇒(p→r), does that mean (p→r)⇒[(p→q)∧(q→r)](p \to r) \Rightarrow [(p \to q) \wedge (q \to r)](p→r)⇒[(p→q)∧(q→r)]? Regrettably, no: consider the case there ppp and rrr are true but qqq is false.

Some rules of inference do work both ways. These are called equivalences.

Definition 2.3.8. If φ↔ψ\varphi \leftrightarrow \psiφ↔ψ is a tautology, then it is an equivalence and φ\varphiφ and ψ\psiψ are equivalent statements. We write φ≡ψ\varphi \equiv \psiφ≡ψ.

Equivalent statements are not the same statements, but they are close enough from the point of view of logic. Equivalent statements are interchangeable, and may be freely substituted with one another.

We will quickly run through many equivalences before slowing down to prove a few.

Distributive properties

In our numbers, multiplication distributes over addition. This is one fact that is likely implemented twice in your head, once for a number: a(b+c)=ab+ac,a(b+c) = ab + ac,a(b+c)=ab+ac, and once for “the negative” (truly the number −1-1−1): −(b+c)=−b−c.-(b+c) = -b - c.−(b+c)=−b−c. However, these rules are one and the same; the distributive law. Likewise, we will have distributive laws for negation, conjunction, and disjunction.

DeMorgan’s law is one of the most important facts in basic logic. In fact, we will see it three times before we are through. In English, DeMorgan’s law says that the negation of a conjunction is a disjunction and vice-versa.

Finally, we come to the properties of conditional (and biconditional) statements. These do not have “obvious” numerical counterparts, but are useful all the same.

Material implication is the surprising result that the conditional is just a jumped-up disjunction! In fact, DeMorgan’s law, material implication, and mutual implication together prove that all of the connectives we’ve studied can be implemented with just two: negation and one other. However, this would make things difficult to read, so we allow the redundancy of five connectives.

Let’s prove material implication with a truth table, and then we will prove contraposition with another method.

Therefore, p→q≡¬p∨qp \to q \equiv \neg p \vee qp→q≡¬p∨q. □\square□

There is another way to think about material implication. The statement p→qp \to qp→q means that ppp never happens without qqq. Another way to say this is that qqq happens, or else ppp did not; so, ¬p∨q\neg p \vee q¬p∨q.

Before we prove contraposition by the other method, we must justify said method with a theorem.

Theorem 2.3.9. Statements φ\varphiφ and ψ\psiψ are equivalent if and only if φ⇒ψ\varphi \Rightarrow \psiφ⇒ψ and ψ⇒φ\psi \Rightarrow \varphiψ⇒φ are both rules of inference.

The phrase “if and only if” in a mathematical statement means that the statement “works both ways.” In this case, φ≡ψ\varphi \equiv \psiφ≡ψ means φ⇒ψ\varphi \Rightarrow \psiφ⇒ψ and ψ⇒φ\psi \Rightarrow \varphiψ⇒φ are both rules of inference; and conversely, φ⇒ψ\varphi \Rightarrow \psiφ⇒ψ and ψ⇒φ\psi \Rightarrow \varphiψ⇒φ both being rules of inference means φ≡ψ\varphi \equiv \psiφ≡ψ. It means that “φ≡ψ\varphi \equiv \psiφ≡ψ” and “”φ⇒ψ\varphi \Rightarrow \psiφ⇒ψ and ψ⇒φ\psi \Rightarrow \varphiψ⇒φ are both rules of inference" are equivalent statements. Fortunately, we already have a rule that justifies this equivalence.

Proof (of Theorem 2.3.9). Mutual implication says that p↔q≡(p→q)∧(q→p)p \leftrightarrow q \equiv (p \to q) \wedge (q \to p)p↔q≡(p→q)∧(q→p). □\square□

It’s that easy. Whenever we have a conditional statement and its converse (the order reversed), we have the biconditional; likewise, we may trade in a biconditional for two converse conditional statements.

Now we will prove contraposition by showing that p→qp\to qp→q implies ¬q→¬p\neg q \to \neg p¬q→¬p, and vice-versa. Before you read the below proof, write down these rules: material implication, double negation, and commutativity of disjunction.

Proof (of contraposition). Suppose ppp and qqq are statements and that p→qp \to qp→q is true. By material implication, ¬p∨q\neg p \vee q¬p∨q is true. Commutativity says this is the same as q∨¬pq \vee \neg pq∨¬p. So, we may apply material implication backwards, negating qqq this time, to get ¬q→¬p\neg q \to \neg p¬q→¬p; therefore, p→qp \to qp→q implies ¬q→¬p\neg q \to \neg p¬q→¬p.

Conversely, suppose ¬q→¬p\neg q \to \neg p¬q→¬p is true. Then material implication (and double negation) gives q∨¬pq \vee \neg pq∨¬p. We may commute the disjunction and apply material implication again, negating ¬p\neg p¬p, to get p→qp \to qp→q.

By mutual implication, p→q≡¬q→¬pp \to q \equiv \neg q \to \neg pp→q≡¬q→¬p. □\square□
