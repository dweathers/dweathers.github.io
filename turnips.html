The surprising result observed is that γ\gammaγ is “pretty close” to π\piπ sometimes. This is ridiculous: π\piπ can be much harder to calculate than γ\gammaγ, and does not even always exist. Why should such an easily-calculated object like γ\gammaγ hit anywhere near π\piπ?

However, this is not ridiculous at all: consider the case of the uniform transition matrix UUU, by which we mean the matrix that equally disperses XXX among its states with equal probability, where Ui,j=1/mU_{i,j} = 1/mUi,j​=1/m always. It is not hard to imagine that γi=1/m\gamma_i = 1/mγi​=1/m and πi=1/m\pi_i = 1/mπi​=1/m for all states iii. So (and Tim observed this immediately) the relationship between γ\gammaγ and π\piπ seems to depend on the “regularity” of the matrix AAA, how “close” it is to the uniform transition matrix.

Proposed definition

I propose the following provisional definition to get a handle on this. First, we need to know what an infinity norm is.

Definition. Let xxx be a vector of finite length. Then its infinity norm (also called the supremum norm or in this case maximum norm) is
∣∣x∣∣∞=max{xi}. ||x||_\infty = \text{max}\{x_i\} .∣∣x∣∣∞​=max{xi​}.

So, the infinity norm is the largest element of the vector.

Definition. Let AAA be the m×mm \times mm×m transition matrix associated to a Markov chain XXX. Let γ\gammaγ be the “normalized column sums” of AAA, i.e. γj=∑iAi,j/m\gamma_j = \sum_{i} A_{i,j}/mγj​=∑i​Ai,j​/m. Let π\piπ be the stationary distribution of AAA, i.e. πi=lim⁡(An)i,j\pi_i = \lim (A^n)_{i,j}πi​=lim(An)i,j​. We will say AAA is kkk-regular when kkk is the largest integer such that
∣∣γ−π∣∣∞<1k. ||\gamma - \pi||_\infty < \frac{1}{k}.∣∣γ−π∣∣∞​<k1​.

For example, if the normalized column sums of AAA predict the stationary distribution with maximum error equal to 5%, then AAA would be 20-regular.

Some questions

A doubly stochastic matrix is one whose columns also sum to 1. (It is baked into the definition that a transition matrix is at least singularly stochastic, i.e. its rows sum to 1.) Is there something that can be said about the kkk-regularity of a doubly stochastic matrix?

Can we get an idea of how likely any transitory matrix with a stationary distribution is to be kkk-regular?

Suppose AAA is a transitory matrix whose stationary distribution does not exist. Can γ\gammaγ give us an idea of the long-term probability of being in one of the states of the Markov chain? (I think the answer to this will be no, at least in the case where XXX is periodic, i.e. will regularly return to the same state every d>1d>1d>1 iterations.)
